## Structure

The article is organized into 4 main parts:

### Part I: Introduction — The Historical Problem
- Frames the Indo-Iranian schism as a civilizational memory preserved in Vedic and Avestan texts
- Introduces the Out of India (OIT) hypothesis as a counter-narrative to Aryan Invasion Theory
- Sets up the three-evidence framework: archaeology, linguistics, mythology

### Part II: The Mitanni Evidence — Indo-Aryans in Mesopotamia  
- Archaeological and textual evidence for Indo-Aryan presence in Mitanni (c. 1400 BCE)
- Analysis of Vedic deity names in Hittite-Mitanni treaties
- Sanskrit terminology in horse-training manuals
- Implications for Indo-Aryan migration patterns

### Part III: The Vedic-Zoroastrian Schism — Memory in Mythology
- Comparative analysis of Vedic and Avestan pantheons
- The inversion of *deva*/*asura* vs. *ahura*/*daeva*
- Textual evidence for a split Indo-Iranian community
- The Baudhāyana Śrautasūtra's migration account

### Part IV: Out of India Theory — Evidence and Controversy
- Archaeological correlates: Sapta Sindhu as homeland
- Linguistic evidence: Sanskrit archaisms and Proto-Indo-European features
- Critique of mainstream Aryan Migration Theory (AMT)
- Scholarly debate and confidence levels

## Key Topics Covered
